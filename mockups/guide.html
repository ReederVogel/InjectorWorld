What is Botox?

Botox is a brand name for onabotulinumtoxinA, a purified protein derived from Clostridium botulinum. In the doses used for aesthetic treatments, it temporarily relaxes specific muscles in the face. The effect is local and predictable. It does not "freeze" your face when administered by a trained injector who knows the anatomy.

Botox was first approved by the FDA in 1989 for medical use and approved for cosmetic use in 2002. It has been studied in millions of patients since. Dysport, Xeomin, and Jeuveau are similar neurotoxins in the same category and are sometimes used interchangeably.

"The single most common reason patients are unhappy with Botox is the injector, not the molecule."Dr. Lena Park, MD · Board-Certified Dermatologist

How it works

Botox blocks the chemical signal between a nerve and a muscle. Without that signal, the muscle cannot contract. The skin above the muscle stops folding into a crease when you make an expression, and over weeks, the existing line softens.

It is temporary by design. Most patients see effects begin 3 to 5 days after injection, peak at 10 to 14 days, and last 3 to 4 months. The body metabolizes the protein and the muscle activity returns to baseline.

A trained injector picks the muscles to treat based on which expressions create which lines on your specific face. Two patients with similar foreheads can need very different treatment maps.

Who is a good candidate?

Most healthy adults between 25 and 65 are candidates. There is no universal "right age." Some patients in their late 20s start preventatively when they notice lines forming during expression. Others wait until their 40s or 50s and treat lines that are already settled.

Botox is not recommended for people who are pregnant or breastfeeding, who have certain neuromuscular conditions (myasthenia gravis, Lambert-Eaton syndrome, ALS), or who have an active infection at the injection site. Your injector should ask about your medical history and current medications before treatment.

What it treats

The most common aesthetic areas are the forehead lines (horizontal lines across the brow), the glabella (the "11s" between the brows), and crow's feet (lines that fan out from the outer corner of the eyes). These three areas account for the majority of first-time Botox treatments.

Beyond those, well-trained injectors treat the masseter muscle (for jaw slimming and TMJ relief), bunny lines on the nose, a downturned mouth, neck bands, and the area above the upper lip (a "lip flip"). Off-label medical uses include excessive sweating (hyperhidrosis), migraines, and bruxism.

"Patients sometimes come in asking for 'Botox' when filler or a peel would actually be the right answer. A good injector will tell you that."Dr. Lena Park, MD

Risks and side effects

Botox is a well-studied treatment with a low complication rate when administered by a trained, licensed injector. That said, every medical treatment carries risk. Here is what to know.

COMMON · RESOLVE IN DAYS
  • ·Bruising at injection site
  • ·Headache for 24 to 48 hours
  • ·Mild swelling or redness
  • ·"Heavy" forehead for 1 to 2 weeks
  • ·Tenderness for 1 to 2 days
RARE · CALL YOUR INJECTOR
  • ·Eyelid or eyebrow drooping (ptosis)
  • ·Asymmetry that does not resolve
  • ·Allergic reaction
  • ·Difficulty swallowing (extremely rare)
  • ·Vision changes (extremely rare)

If you experience any of the rare side effects, contact your injector immediately. Most complications are resolvable when caught early.

What to expect at your appointment

A typical first appointment takes 30 to 45 minutes. The consultation is the longest part. Your injector will ask about your goals, study your face at rest and in expression, and recommend a unit count and a treatment map. The injections themselves take 5 to 10 minutes.

The needles used for Botox are very small. Most patients describe a quick pinch. Numbing cream is available but rarely necessary. Mild bruising is the most common side effect and can be reduced by avoiding blood thinners for 5 to 7 days before treatment.

Aftercare

Stay upright for 4 hours. Skip strenuous exercise and saunas for 24 hours. Avoid touching, rubbing, or applying pressure to the treated areas for 24 hours so the product stays where it was placed. You can return to normal makeup and skincare the next day.

You will start to feel the effect at 3 to 5 days. Final result is at 14 days. Schedule any follow-up tweaks for the 2-week mark, not before. Plan your next session for 12 to 16 weeks out.

Frequently asked questions

How long does Botox last? +
Typically 3 to 4 months. Some patients metabolize it faster, some slower. After several consistent treatments, many patients find the effect lasts a bit longer.
Does Botox hurt? +
Most patients rate the discomfort as a 2 or 3 out of 10. The needles are very fine. A quick pinch at each injection site. The whole process is over in minutes.
Can I exercise after Botox? +
Wait 24 hours. Strenuous exercise increases blood flow and can displace the product before it settles. Light walking is fine the same day.
What's the difference between Botox, Dysport, and Xeomin? +
All three are botulinum toxin type A. Dysport tends to kick in faster and may spread slightly more. Xeomin has no protective protein, which some patients prefer if they have developed resistance to Botox. Effects, duration, and price are broadly similar.
Will Botox make me look "frozen"? +
No, when administered correctly. A frozen look comes from too many units in the wrong placement. A well-trained injector will use fewer units in carefully chosen sites so you can still express. Communicate your preference clearly in the consult.
Is Botox safe long-term? +
The aesthetic doses are very small relative to medical doses used for migraines or spasticity, where patients have been treated for decades. The long-term safety profile is well established. Most concerns relate to muscle atrophy with prolonged use, which is mild and reversible when treatments stop.
